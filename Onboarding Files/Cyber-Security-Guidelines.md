# Cyber Security

## Cybersecurity Threats

Common cybersecurity threats are phishing attacks, weak passwords, unsecured Wi-Fi networks, and malware. The following threats cause problems working in remote settings, for phishing attacks, users sometimes forget or zone out on checking emails and messages. As for the weak password, people tend to use the same password for every account they have. Lastly, for the unsecured Wi-Fi networks, people tend to use free Wi-Fi, especially in cafes or restaurants, which can be a threat to your PC's

## Best Practices

Always a great practice is to check for any updates on your Operating System and check for security updates as well to solidify security on your devices. Another way is to construct strong and unique passwords for each of your accounts in order to reduce the threat of being hacked. Lastly, use a multi-factor authenticator to securely access your account more.

## Securing your Computer

It is important to secure your computer so that other people cannot easily steal sensitive data. This also prevents unauthorized actions this reduces malicious people using your devices to send emails or install malware. In order to secure data you need to add a password to your device in order to not easily use your laptop or PC anytime.

## Handling Phishing and Suspicious Emails

In order to handle phishing and suspicious email is to, stay calm and act quickly. Don't interact with the website or download any files, and disconnect from the internet to prevent malware from stealing the data. Lastly, scan your device using an antivirus or anti-malware software to detect and remove threats.

## Strong Password

Using a password manager can make your password stronger and more unique. It makes a strong password when you create it uniquely and using different password for every account you have. Using a password manager can create a unique password for you and can make your account secure.

## 2 Factor Authenticator

2 Factor Authenticator is important due to its ability to verify the user if it is truly you who is signing in to the account. Having the authenticator makes your login secure and can reduce the chances of hacking. Enabling this authenticator is for accounts that are private, such as online bank accounts, emails, and company accounts. This solidifies your account from possible threat intrusions.

# Proof

I have enabled Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) across my primary work and development accounts, including GitHub, and my professional email. To maintain a high security standard, I use a TOTP (Time-based One-Time Password) method via an authenticator app rather than SMS, ensuring that my recovery codes and sensitive access points remain protected even in the event of a SIM-swapping attempt.

I am currently using Bitwarden as my primary password manager to store and generate unique, high-entropy credentials for my internship and personal portfolio projects. By centralizing my login data in an encrypted vault, I’ve moved away from password reuse and have secured the vault itself with a strong master password and biometric unlocking for my mobile devices.

Regarding my device security, I have configured my laptop to automatically lock after 5 minutes of inactivity to protect my source code and sensitive documents when I am away from my desk. My phone is set to a more aggressive 30-second screen timeout and requires a fingerprint scan or a 6-digit PIN for every wake-up, ensuring my hardware remains inaccessible to unauthorized users in shared environments.

The one new cybersecurity habit I am implementing at Focus Bear is a "Pre-Commit Secret Sweep" before every repository push. This habit involves a dedicated check of my .env files and API keys to ensure no sensitive credentials—like database strings or private tokens—accidentally leak into my version control history, protecting the integrity of both my professional and personal development work.
