# When to Use Google, AI Tools, or Ask for Help?

My flowchart is on [https://github.com/JuztisLeague/juztisleague-intern-repo/blob/main/Onboarding%20Files/Decision%20Mkaing%20Flowchart%20on%20AI%20Tools.jpg](url)

I prefer using AI when I need a quick, direct explanation of a concept or when I am stuck on a specific error and need help understanding what went wrong and how to fix it. AI is very useful when I need someone to walk me through a solution step by step in simple terms, especially when I am a beginner, and Google results are too technical or scattered across multiple pages. For example, during my internship, when I kept getting the Provider error in my Redux tests, using AI helped me understand not just the fix but also why the error was happening in the first place. On the other hand, I prefer using Google when I need the most up-to-date information, official documentation, or when I want to see multiple different perspectives and solutions from the developer community. Google is also better when I need to find a specific library, check release notes, or read through Stack Overflow discussions where many developers have encountered the same problem. In general, AI is better for understanding and explaining, while Google is better for researching and finding the most current information.

I decide to ask a colleague when I have already spent a reasonable amount of time trying to solve the problem on my own using AI and Google, and I am still stuck or going in circles. Asking a colleague is especially important when the problem is related to something specific to the project or the codebase that only someone on the team would know about, such as a specific architectural decision, a team convention, or a business requirement. It is also better to ask a colleague when the problem is blocking me from making progress on a task that affects the rest of the team, because spending too long stuck on one issue alone can slow down the whole project. I also ask a colleague when I am unsure if my solution is the right approach for the project, even if it technically works, because a more experienced team member can tell me if there is a better or more standard way to do it.

Developers face several challenges when troubleshooting alone. The biggest challenge is tunnel vision, which happens when you have been staring at the same problem for so long that you stop seeing obvious solutions and keep going in the same direction even when it is not working. This is why a fresh pair of eyes from a colleague can often spot the problem immediately, even though you missed it after hours of looking. Another challenge is not knowing what you do not know, which means sometimes the real cause of the problem is something you have never encountered before, and you do not even know what to search for. This makes it very hard to find the right solution on your own. Time management is also a challenge because it is easy to spend too much time on one problem when you are alone, which affects your overall productivity and can cause you to miss deadlines. Debugging asynchronous code and network-related issues is particularly difficult alone because the errors are often not straightforward and require understanding how multiple parts of the system interact. Finally, without a colleague to validate your solution, you might fix the immediate error but introduce a new problem or use an approach that does not follow the team's standards, which can create bigger issues down the line.
