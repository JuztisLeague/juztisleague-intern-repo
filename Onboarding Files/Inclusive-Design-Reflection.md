# Working with Vulnerable Populations

## Vulnerable Populations

People who are part of vulnerable populations are those who are low-income individuals, older adults, and racial and ethnic minorities. These people or groups are more susceptible to digital exclusion due to intersecting social, economic, and structural disadvantages. The challenges they usually face in the digital space are limited access to internet connectivity, devices, and affordable services. Another challenge is the low digital literacy, which shows the lack of skills in navigating through online platforms effectively, and lastly, safety and privacy risk.

## Ethical Consideration

When designing a website or application for a neurodivergent individual must be ethically considerate in the creation of the application. One way of doing this is to avoid sensory overload, which you must minimize the visual, auditory, and tactile triggers such as flashing animations, loud sounds, or cluttered layouts. Another suggestion is to ensure clear and respectful communication, where you must use simple and direct language without jargon, which provides clarity of the words and phrases being used. Lastly, the person using the application has the capability to customize and edit the application from the font size, color themes, navigation paths, and interaction methods.

## Accessible Content and Interaction

The use of simple language and clear instructions is your foundation for accessibility. In short, you must make it simple for them to quickly understand your information being portrayed. Be consistent with the UI/UX design in order for the users to navigate the application quickly and efficiently.  Lastly, you must support focus and task completion, which shows the progress of the user, which is what the Focus Bear was intended to do.

## Support Neurodivergent Individuals

In order to support neurodivergent individuals in the workspace, you must use clear communication, which involves using direct and specific language. Another way is to respect for there different working styles, which is to recognize those people of there unique way of adapting to the workspace. Lastly is to be flexible, which means to find a way to suit there strength and provide a quiet and sensory-friendly workspace.

# Reflection

In communicating with neurodivergent users or teammates, I realized that I must adjust the way I express ideas. I should construct my phrases more simply and clearly so that instructions and explanations are easier to understand. Many neurodivergent individuals, particularly those with ADHD or Autism, may experience cognitive overload when information is presented in complex or overly technical language. Because of this, clear and concise communication is important to help them understand tasks quickly and reduce confusion.

A first-person account that helped me understand this better came from a user with ADHD discussing productivity apps on Reddit. The user shared that they often spend hours organizing tasks and customizing the productivity tool instead of actually completing tasks. They explained that their brain enjoys the “setup phase,” but once everything is organized, their motivation disappears. This shows how too many options or complicated setups can create a distraction instead of helping productivity.

Another issue mentioned by people with ADHD is decision paralysis. When an application shows too many tasks or options, users may feel overwhelmed and avoid using the tool entirely. This highlights the importance of designing productivity tools that reduce cognitive load and present information in a simple and manageable way.

Based on my experience using Focus Bear, the application has helped me improve my focus and productivity. The concept of guiding users through routines and blocking distractions is helpful. However, some aspects can still be difficult for new users to understand because they must discover certain features on their own.

One suggestion is the addition of a simple user manual or a guided onboarding system. This could include a beginner-friendly tutorial or a step-by-step walkthrough when users first install the app. For example, the onboarding system could show users how to:

- Create a focus session
- Set up a routine
- Configure distractions blocking
- Understand how the productivity system works

Providing this kind of guidance would help reduce confusion and allow users to use the app more effectively from the start.

Another potential improvement is implementing a Low-Energy Mode for users experiencing executive dysfunction. In this mode, the app could show only a few small tasks instead of a long list. For example, instead of displaying a large task like “Clean the kitchen,” the system could break it down into smaller steps, such as:

- Put one dish in the sink
- Wipe one section of the counter
- Throw away trash

Breaking tasks into smaller steps can help users start tasks more easily and avoid feeling overwhelmed.

It is also important to communicate with empathy when users struggle with productivity. Many neurodivergent users experience executive functioning difficulties, meaning that starting tasks, organizing priorities, or maintaining focus can be challenging.

A supportive response to a struggling user could look like this:

" _**Hi there,**_

_**Thank you for sharing how you’re feeling. Many people experience difficulty getting started on tasks, especially when they feel overwhelmed or mentally tired. You’re not alone in feeling this way.**_

_**One thing that can help is starting with something very small. Instead of trying to complete an entire task, try focusing on just one tiny step. For example, instead of cleaning an entire room, start by picking up one item. Small steps can build momentum and make larger tasks feel more manageable.**_

_**If Focus Bear feels overwhelming right now, you could try using it for just one small goal today, such as a short focus session. The goal is not perfection, but progress. If you need help simplifying your routines, we would be happy to assist you.**_

_**Take care and remember that progress can happen one step at a time.**_ "

This type of communication helps reduce shame or frustration and encourages users to take manageable steps toward productivity.
